WHO WE ARE

KEY MESSAGE:
m2m is a proudly African, female-focused not-for profit. We unequivocally believe that health is a human right, and we are demonstrating how investing in local African changemakers can deliver health for all and advance universal health coverage by the UN’s deadline of 2030.  

KEY m2m FACTS

KEY EXTERNAL FACTS

We are proudly African. From humble beginnings at a single site in Cape Town, we now work from hundreds of locations across 10 African countries, having reached over 15 million women, children, adolescents, and families since 2001 across sub-Saharan Africa. Our head office is in Cape Town, South Africa and we invest in and hire local talent, building the economic resilience of the communities we work with. 

We are tackling some of our generation’s most pressing issues. HIV remains a leading cause of death, and— together with life-threatening related conditions like tuberculosis (TB), malaria, cervical cancer, and diabetes—has created a health crisis, with vulnerable women and children the most affected.  (see facts in “key challenges” above) 

Our work across the continent is driven by our frontline team of local African women.  Since our creation in 2001, 12,000 women have been trained and employed by m2m as CHWs. They work alongside doctors and nurses in facilities and communities as professionalised members of the health care system to support their peers get the health care they need and deserve. 

Everyone, everywhere deserves to access the health care they need and deserve. And yet: 

- More than half of the people on the continent—around 615 million—are unable to access basic health care services.   

- More than 70% of African countries still face critical shortages – with needs-based estimates showing that the African Region will need between 5.3 and 6.1 million additional health workers by 2030. (WHO) 

m2m is committed to breaking barriers to health care and delivering health for all and leaving no one behind, achieving health equity, and advancing the UN Sustainable Development Goals, in particular goals 3 (health care), 5 (gender equity), 8 (decent work), and 17 (partnerships). We believe in the power and promise of UHC for all Africans, with CHWs at the heart of delivering health for all.

WHAT WE DO

KEY MESSAGE:
m2m delivers and enables integrated primary health services for families, creating health for all and leaving no one behind, using a proven, community-focused and -led model, at a time when more than half of the African people are unable to access basic health services.  

KEY m2m FACTS

KEY EXTERNAL FACTS

A simple and yet powerful solution—the Mentor Mother Model.   

We meet the most pressing health needs of the communities we serve, ensuring people closest to challenges design and deliver solutions for long-term.  

The magic of our model is that it is peer-based and local: integrated services are delivered by people from the same community as those they serve—meaning they are rooted in shared realities and experiences. 

Despite increases in the global health workforce, numbers remain low in regions with the highest burden of disease, including sub-Saharan Africa, where the African Union has called for 2 more million CHWs to be trained, recruited, and deployed.  

Between 2014 and 2021, sub-Saharan Africa continually had the lowest health worker density, with only 2.3 medical doctors and 12.6 nursing and midwifery personnel per 10,000 population. In contrast, Europe had the highest density for doctors, at 39.4 per 10,000 population, while Northern America had the most nursing and midwifery personnel, with 152.1 per 10,000 population. (UN, p21)

By 2030, Africa will face a shortage of six million health workers, making CHWs a key component and a fast solution to bridging the continent’s health needs. (IntraHealth/WHO) 

Integrated primary health care is the key to achieving health for all. That is why, alongside HIV services, almost 12,000 m2m CHWs have been trained and equipped since our creation in 2001to deliver additional health services—including education, screening, and treatment—for diseases such as cervical cancer, tuberculosis, diabetes, and malaria. All of these diseases disproportionately affect people living with HIV.  

We successfully piloted new service offerings to tackle these health challenges and are now bringing them to scale across the ten Africa countries we work in.

Our health facility-to-community platform is designed to ensure over 1 million women, children, and adolescents access health care, start any treatment they need, and are retained in care every year. 

Mentor Mothers are a vital link between health centres and their surrounding communities, connecting, and keeping, people in care—two elements that are critical to providing the whole-of-society approach that is needed for primary health care to effectively organise and strengthen national health systems to bring services for health and wellbeing closer to communities. In 2022, m2m achieved a 100% retention rate at 3 months and 99% at 6 months among m2m clients newly initiated on HIV-treatment. 

Working hand in hand with doctors, nurses and Mentor Mothers at health centres, Community Mentor Mothers go door-to-door in the catchment areas served by the nearest health centre to deliver services, as well as educate, engage, and follow up with clients who have not received medical care or who have stopped treatment. Mentor Mothers based at nearby health centres can then support clients to access the services they need. Digital tools strengthen the community and health centre connection, ensuring accurate tracking, follow–up, and reporting.

Heath care and other sectors need to work together to ensure all decisions affecting health are addressed in an integrated way.

HOW WE DO IT

KEY EXTERNAL FACTS

We believe and invest in local and female community leadership. 

Why? Because professional CHWs—such as m2m Mentor Mothers—delivering integrated PHC services are critical to unlocking health for all. The international consensus is clear: CHWs + PHC = UHC. CHWs are therefore key drivers of PHC, and of our journey towards health for all.   

That is why m2m has insisted on investing in the training of and paying Mentor Mothers for more than 22 years, long before other organisations started talking about this.

This includes “providing care in the community as well as care through the community” (WHO).

Family-centric approach to health care.  

Through our Mentor Mother Model, we serve entire families and entire communities—women, children, adolescents, and special populations.   

Our impact begins with one woman connecting with another, understanding her unique health needs and that of her family, and then working together to meet those.

Retention in care statistics.   

A peer reviewed article from 2019 looked at the impact of m2m’s Mentor Mother Model on retention in care of mother-baby pairs in the East Central region of Uganda. Sexual and reproductive health rights  

While strides have been made, too many women are still denied the autonomy to decide what is best for them. And yet, women have shown the power they have to be key agents of change in their communities, and beyond, and should be acknowledged as the vital partners in creating healthy and thriving societies that they truly are.   

Only 30% of women aged 15 to 49 who are married or in a union in SSA are able to make decisions about their sexual and reproductive health and rights, according to data collected from 68 countries for 2007–2022, compared to over 80% cent in some countries in Europe and Latin America and the Caribbean. (UN, p.24) 

That is why m2m is supporting girls and women to take ownership of their own choices and future through health education, mentoring, and job opportunities in public health.  

Lack of investment and fair working conditions for CHWs 

The WHO estimates that by 2030, there will be a shortfall of 4.2 million health workers for the African continent (WHO, p44).  

But according to Africa Frontline First, the African continent is currently facing an annual financing gap toward community health of around $4.4 billion.   

And only 14% of Community Health Workers are salaried in sub-Saharan Africa. What is more, despite making up 70% of the health workforce, only 25% women are in leadership positions (WHO).  

Fair compensation for CHWs is not just ethical, it helps to improve impact through increased commitment and focus from CHWs. And it makes business sense—an international study suggests that each $1 invested in CHW programmes delivers a $10 return.  

Working towards a global roadmap that delivers health for all.  

Universal health coverage (UHC) means that all people have access to the full range of quality health services they need, when and where they need them, without financial hardship. It covers the full continuum of essential health services, from health promotion to prevention, treatment, rehabilitation and palliative care. 

But the world is off track to make significant progress towards UHC (Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) target 3.8) by 2030. Improvements to health services coverage have stagnated since 2015, and the proportion of the population that faced catastrophic levels of out-of-pocket health spending increased continuously since 2000. This global pattern is consistent across all regions and the majority of countries. (WHO) 

To build back better, WHO’s recommendation is to reorient health systems using a primary health care approach—which m2m’s 2022-2026 Strategic Plan fully aligns with. Most (90%) of essential UHC interventions can be delivered through a PHC approach, potentially saving 60 million lives and increasing average global life expectancy by 3.7 years by 2030. (WHO) 

Creating sustainable and strong health systems contributes to the attainment of the goal of Health for All by 2030 and responds to the needs of communities. 

That is why robust and adequately funded health systems, underpinned by CHWs, like m2m Mentor Mothers, who are protected, supported, and salaried, are a prerequisite to building health security, attainment of global goals and UHC aspirations.  

However, the underfunding of health systems, fragmentation of health financing schemes, and inefficiency in resource allocation and use are persistent challenges. Health systems in Africa are 80% efficient implying that almost one in every five dollars spent on health is lost to technical inefficiency (WHO).  

Paid, professional, and formally recognised CHWs not only ensure no one is left behind or lost to care—they also relieve tremendous pressures on already overstretched and understaffed health systems.

Impact of Climate change  

The climate crisis is a health crisis—and one that affects vulnerable women and girls at disproportionate levels. Indeed, it is a crisis that is far from being gender neutral: Only a third of countries include sexual and reproductive health in their national plans to tackle the climate crisis (UNFPA). Rising temperatures have been linked to poorer maternal health and complications during pregnancy, such as gestational diabetes. Extreme heat has been associated with triggering earlier deliveries and an increase in stillbirths. 

The climate crisis has notably underscored the urgency of improving access to care and building health systems that can adapt to a changing environment. And robust health systems are founded on a well-supported health workforce, of which CHWs are the foundation. As trusted members of the community and a link between the health system and individuals, CHWs are essential in both responding to the impacts of climate change and promoting community resilience to climate change. 

Other ways in which climate change impacts women's health: 

- Water and Food Insecurity, and economic livelihoods: disrupted agricultural patterns lead to food shortages and increased food prices. Women are also often responsible for securing water and food for their families, putting them at higher risk of malnutrition and related health problems. 

- Vector-Borne Diseases: Changes in temperature and precipitation patterns can influence the distribution and prevalence of vector-borne diseases like malaria, dengue fever, and Zika virus. Women, especially pregnant women, may face increased risks from these diseases. 

- Increased Vulnerability in Displacement Scenarios:  Women often face higher risks of violence, exploitation, and inadequate healthcare in situations of forced displacement, which can adversely affect their physical and mental well-being. 

- Air Pollution: Climate change exacerbates air pollution, which can have significant health impacts, particularly on women's respiratory health. Indoor air pollution from cooking with biomass fuels disproportionately affects women in many developing countries. 

According to the World Economic Forum, by 2050 the climate crisis may lead to: 
- Over 2 billion healthy life years lost,
